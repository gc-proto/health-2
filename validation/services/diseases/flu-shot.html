Everyone 6 months or older should get the flu shot

The flu shot is your best defence against the flu. It can save lives by:

  • protecting you if you are exposed to the virus
  • preventing you from getting very sick
  • helping protect other people because you are less likely to spread the virus to others

Where to get your flu shot

People who especially need the flu shot

Those who are at high risk of complications from the flu:

  • People with health conditions such as:
    • cancer
    • diabetes
    • heart disease
    • lung disease
    • obesity
  • People 65 years and older
  • People who live in nursing homes or other long-term care facilities
  • Children under 5 years of age
  • Pregnant women or those planning a pregnancy
  • Indigenous peoples

People who can pass the virus on to people at high risk also need the flu shot:

  • Family and other household members
  • Child care workers
  • Caregivers
  • Health care workers
  • Those who provide services to people at high risk in closed settings, such as crew on a ship

Get a flu shot every year

It's important that you get a new flu vaccine every year because:

  • effectiveness of the vaccine can wear off, so you need a new one every year to stay protected
  • the type of flu virus usually changes from year to year
  • experts create a new vaccine to protect you each flu season
  • there's evidence that repeated annual vaccinations can provide more protection against severe outcomes

The flu vaccine is safe and effective

  • You can't get the flu from the flu vaccine
  • Most people have no reaction to the flu vaccine
  • Severe reactions are very rare

2017-2018 flu vaccine

Each year, the viruses that are most likely to cause flu are identified in advance and vaccines are made to match them as closely as possible. The vaccines are recommended by the World Health Organization (WHO).

This season's flu vaccine will protect against the influenza viruses that research indicates will be most common during the season.

This year's vaccine includes:

  • A(H1N1)
  • A(H3N2)
  • Influenza B

Health professionals: get more information on the specific vaccine:

  • 2017-2018 Seasonal influenza vaccine pocket guide
  • National Advisory Committee on Immunization (NACI): Canadian Immunization Guide Chapter on Influenza and Statement on Seasonal Influenza Vaccine for 2017-2018
