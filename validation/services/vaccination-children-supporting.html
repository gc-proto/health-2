Before the vaccination

Remember to take your child's vaccination record with you when you visit your healthcare provider or public health office (CLSC in Quebec). If you don't have a vaccination record, be sure to ask for one.

If your child is sick or has a fever when it's time for their vaccination, talk to your healthcare provider. They can assess whether to give the vaccination or if it should wait until your child is no longer sick.

During the vaccination

Your health care provider may ask you questions about your child's health, such as if they have allergies or health problems.

There are several things you can do to help your child during the vaccination, such as:

  • relax and stay positive
    • your child may react to your emotions
  • cuddle and talk to your child during the vaccination as this can help reduce or prevent crying
  • if you are breastfeeding, try nursing your baby right before, during or after the needle
  • distract your child by talking to them, giving them a favourite toy or even singing
  • reward your child with something they enjoy after getting their shot
    • this is effective on older children

After the vaccination

Most children are fine after vaccination and have no reaction to the vaccine. In some cases, your child may:

  • be fussy
  • have a low fever
  • be sleepier than usual
  • have a sore, swollen, or red spot where the needle went in

These reactions are normal. They mean the body is doing its job to learn and fight the germs from the vaccine. Discomfort can last between 12 and 24 hours.

You can give your child medicine to help with the pain or lower the fever. Ask your healthcare provider what medicine is best.

Before you go home

Make an appointment for your child's next vaccination.

You will be asked to wait at the clinic for 15 to 20 minutes after your child's vaccination. This is because, as with any medicine, there is a very slight chance of a serious allergic reaction (anaphylaxis).

Signs of a serious allergic reaction include:

  • swelling of the face
  • breathing problems (wheezing)
  • blotchy skin on the body (hives)

If you see any of these symptoms, talk to a healthcare provider right away. They know what to do to counter the allergic reaction.

When to call your health care provider

Serious reactions to vaccines are very rare. Call your health care provider or public health office (CLSC in Quebec) if your child has unusual symptoms after vaccination.

Unusual symptoms may include:

  • unusual sleepiness.
  • a fever above 40°C (104°F)
  • crying or fussing for more than 24 hours
  • worsening swelling where the needle went in

You know your child best. If you notice anything that is not normal after a vaccination, check with your healthcare provider.

Checklist for your child's vaccinations

There are 4 main things for you do to for your child’s vaccinations:

  1. Make an appointment
  2. Bring your child's vaccination record to the appointment
    1. You will get this at your first appointment. If your healthcare provider forgets, be sure to ask for one.
  3. Make the next appointment before you leave your healthcare provider’s office
  4. Mark the next appointment in your cell phone or home calendar to help you remember

Keep your child's vaccination record in a safe place, so you can find it when you need it. From time to time, proof of your child's vaccinations may be requested.

In some parts of Canada, children need to have all of their vaccinations up-to-date before starting school or daycare. Your child's vaccination history is helpful if you take your child to another healthcare provider or travel outside Canada.

Remember, vaccination is part of your children's routine care. Keeping vaccinations up-to-date is important to protect their health.
