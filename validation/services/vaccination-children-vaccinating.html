Vaccines are monitored and tested

Like all medicines, vaccines need to go through a series of tests before use in Canada. Many systems are in place to monitor the making, use and safety of vaccines.

Health care providers report vaccine reactions to local public health authorities. This is to make sure unusual or unexpected reactions are dealt with quickly.

For more information, watch our vaccine safety video.

Vaccines do not cause autism

Scientists have not found a link between vaccines and autism.

In the mid to late 1990s, researchers from the United Kingdom reported an association between MMR vaccine and inflammatory bowel disease, and MMR vaccine and autism. Rigorous scientific studies and reviews of the evidence have been done worldwide, and there is now considerable evidence to refute those claims.

In 2010, the original study suggesting a link between the MMR vaccine and autism was found to be fraudulent and was retracted.

Consult these references for more information:

  • Measles, Mumps and Rubella (MMR) Vaccine Safety Studies – Centers for Disease Control and Prevention
  • Immunization Action Coalition: MMR vaccine does not cause autism

Your child's immune system can handle vaccines

Your child's immune system is able to handle the weak or dead germs in a vaccine. Your child may have side effects that last a few days after vaccination, but these should not disrupt daily activities. Common side effects include mild fever or a sore arm.

If your child is not vaccinated and catches the real disease, the result can be serious or even fatal. This is because active germs multiply quickly and your child's immune system is not prepared to defend itself.

Early vaccination protects your child

The vaccination schedule is designed to protect your child before they are exposed to vaccine-preventable diseases. Children are vaccinated early in life because this is when they are vulnerable to diseases.

If they are not vaccinated early, the consequences can be very serious. However, if vaccinated on time, your child has the most protection as early as possible.

Your child may need the same vaccination more than once

Your child needs to be vaccinated at several stages in order to be fully protected. Some vaccines need to be given more than once to build up your child's immune system over time.

Vaccination schedules could be different, depending on the province or territory you live in. This means that some provinces or territories will give the same vaccine at different ages.

Your healthcare provider will give you a vaccination schedule that tells you which vaccines your child needs, at what age.

Typically, your child will be vaccinated:

  • between birth or age 2 months
  • at 4 months
  • at 6 months
  • between 12 months and 18 months
  • between 4 to 6 years of age

School aged children will require more vaccines.

Get your child's vaccination schedule

Multiple vaccines don't overload your child's immune system

Some vaccines are given together to protect against several diseases at once. Your child's immune system can easily handle more than one vaccine at a time safely and effectively.

Your healthcare provider will let you know which vaccines your child needs at each visit.
