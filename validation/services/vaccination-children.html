Protecting your child in the 21st century

Diseases that were once common among children are now rare in Canada because of vaccines. However, they still exist and spread around the world.

Even 1 case of measles can spread quickly when people are not vaccinated. It is not easy to tell who is carrying the germ, or if your child has been exposed.

Many diseases that can be prevented with vaccines have no treatment or cure. In some cases, children can die from complications of a disease.

The best protection for your children against these diseases is to prevent them. The best method to prevent diseases is to keep vaccinating.

Diseases that can be prevented with vaccines*

Chickenpox (Varicella)

Symptoms

  • fever
  • many small blisters that develop scabs
  • itching

Possible complications

  • ear infection
  • pneumonia
  • skin infection (e.g. impetigo), sometimes severe (e.g. flesh-eating disease)
  • encephalitis (brain infection)
  • malformations in infants in cases where mother was infected during pregnancy
  • shingles (15-30% over lifetime)
  • death
Diphtheria

Symptoms

  • high fever
  • severe sore throat
  • respiratory and heart problems

Possible complications

  • paralysis
  • death in 5-10% of cases
German Measles (Rubella)

Symptoms

  • rash
  • swollen glands
  • arthritis (especially in women)

Possible complications

  • miscarriage in pregnant women
  • malformations in infants in cases where mother was infected during pregnancy
Haemophilus Influenzae type B (Hib)

Symptoms

  • pneumonia
  • epiglottitis (severe swelling of the throat)
  • meningitis (infection of the lining of the brain)

Possible complications

  • deafness
  • intellectual and developmental disabilities
  • death (5% of meningitis cases)
Hepatitis B

Symptoms

  • fever
  • diarrhea
  • vomiting
  • abdominal pain
  • jaundice (yellow colouring of the skin and the whites of the eyes)

Possible complications

  • severe liver disease
  • long-term liver infection (10% of adults and up to 90% of infants)
  • cirrhosis of the liver
  • liver cancer
  • death (1% of cases)
Measles

Symptoms

  • rash
  • cough
  • fever
  • conjunctivitis (pink eye)
  • general feeling of illness

Possible complications

  • ear infection (5-9% of cases)
  • pneumonia (1-5% of cases)
  • convulsions
  • permanent brain damage (1 case per 1,000)
  • death (1 case per 3,000)
Meningococcal

Symptoms

  • high fever
  • severe headache
  • nausea and vomiting
  • general feeling of illness
  • red marks or tiny pin-size hemorrhages or bruises on the skin

Possible complications

  • permanent brain damage
  • amputation of hands or feet (10-15% of those infected with serogroup C)
  • death (10-15% of individuals infected with serogroup C)
Mumps

Symptoms

  • fever
  • headache
  • swollen glands near jawbone

Possible complications

  • meningitis (10-30% of cases)
  • deafness
  • testicular infection
  • ovarian infection
Pneumococcal

Symptoms

  • fever
  • cough
  • fatigue
  • headache
  • muscle and joint pain
  • general feeling of illness

Possible complications

  • ear infection
  • sinusitis
  • bronchitis
  • pneumonia
  • death
Polio

Symptoms

  • fever
  • nausea and vomiting
  • general discomfort
  • paralysis of arms and legs (1% of cases)

Possible complications

  • breathing problems
  • permanent paralysis (nearly 50% of hospitalized cases)
  • death (5% of hospitalized cases)
Seasonal Flu (Influenza)

Symptoms

  • fever
  • cough
  • fatigue
  • headache
  • muscle and joint pain
  • general feeling of illness

Possible complications

  • death
  • sinusitis
  • bronchitis
  • pneumonia
  • ear infection
Tetanus

Symptoms

  • jaw spasms
  • vocal chord spasms
  • full-body muscle spasms

Possible complications

  • death in 10% of cases
Whooping Cough (Pertussis)

Symptoms

  • violent coughing fits that may persist for months
  • difficulty eating, drinking and breathing

Possible complications

  • pneumonia
  • convulsions
  • brain damage (1 case per 11,000)
  • death (0.4% among infants)

*Content adapted from the Canadian Paediatric Society's "Your Child's Best Shot - A parent's guide to vaccination."

Vaccines explained

Vaccines are made with a tiny amount of dead or weakened germs (even less than what’s on a dirty toy). These germs help the immune system learn how to protect itself against disease.

Most vaccines are given by injection (a needle) into your child's upper arm or thigh. Some vaccines can be given orally (by mouth) or nasally (sprayed into the nose).

Your child can safely get more than one vaccine at a time. Some vaccines protect against several diseases in a single shot, while others are given separately.

How vaccines work

Your body’s immune system protects you from germs, like bacteria and viruses that cause diseases. The dead or weakened germs in vaccines help your child's immune system make two important tools: antibodies and immune memory.

Together, these tools help your child’s body recognize and fight off the germs if exposed to them in the future. If not vaccinated, your child can get very sick from the germs of vaccine-preventable diseases because the immune system is not prepared.

Thanks to vaccination, most children are fully protected after they are vaccinated. This means that they will never get serious vaccine-preventable diseases.

In rare cases, children who are vaccinated can still get the disease because they only get partial protection from the vaccine. This is more common in children with a health problem that affects their immune system.

These children may develop mild symptoms if they are exposed to a disease, but will not suffer serious issues. If you are worried your child may have immune issues or allergies to a vaccine, ask your healthcare provider for testing.
